# AI Governance Isn't Boring — It's the Most Important UX Problem

Here's the premise of PublicAI Pulse:

**The people who design AI governance systems are making UX decisions.**

They're choosing who gets to interact with the system, what feedback they receive, how transparent the system is about its reasoning. These are UX decisions. We just don't call them that.

## The Slider Problem

PublicAI Pulse is a browser-based simulator. It has two sliders:

1. **Transparency** — how much of the AI's reasoning is visible to affected parties
2. **Bias Correction Intensity** — how aggressively the system attempts to correct for historical biases

Move these sliders and watch what happens to simulated healthcare access, transit routing, housing allocation.

Building this taught me: **the slider positions are not neutral**. Every deployment of an AI system in public services has implicit slider settings — usually set by engineers who never had to explain them to anyone.

## Why Governance Looks Boring (And Isn't)

Governance looks boring because it's discussed in the language of policy. This language obscures what governance actually *is*: decisions about power. About who gets to know what. About who gets to appeal when something goes wrong.

That's UX. That's design. That's governance.

## Design Principles for AI Governance

1. **Legibility** — Affected parties should understand what factors the system considers
2. **Contestability** — There must be a real mechanism for challenging decisions
3. **Disparity monitoring** — Outcomes must be tracked by demographic group
4. **Sunset clauses** — Systems should require active renewal, not passive continuation
5. **Participatory auditing** — Affected communities should have representatives in audit processes

None of these are technically difficult. They're all design decisions.

We're at an inflection point. The governance decisions made in the next five years will shape who benefits and who is harmed. These decisions look like policy. They're really design.
